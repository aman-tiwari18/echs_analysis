Executive Summary

Module 14 examines the pre-authorization control: the gap between what a hospital declares (and ECHS sanctions) before treatment and what it actually bills afterwards. Over the last five years, 142,200 claims passed through the live unlisted-procedure pre-auth channel with both a sanction and a final bill. They were sanctioned for ₹1,044.58 Cr but billed ₹2,143.68 Cr — an overall 2.05× ratio and ₹1,276.37 Cr billed above sanction.

Immediate recommended actions
  1. Audit the top-excess hospitals (723 logins ≥20 claims) — reconcile a sample of bills against their pre-auth sanctions and CGHS package rates.
  2. Introduce partial sanction / CGHS benchmarking at Stage 2 — today the sanction simply equals the hospital's own estimate, so there is no financial gate.
  3. Investigate the steepest bill/sanction ratios — they reached 2.07× on average and far higher per claim.
  4. Recover demonstrable overbilling where the actual bill cannot be justified against the sanctioned, documented procedure.
How the analysis works
Parameters & Methodology

Data. The live pre-auth system (unlisted_procedure, Dec 2021 onward) joined to its claim (claim_intimation for the treating hospital via office master, claim_submission for the actual billed and UTI-approved amounts). Revised pre-auth rows are deduplicated to the latest per (claim, procedure), then summed per claim.

Five-stage pipeline. (1) hospital submits a pre-auth request with a cost estimate → (2) ECHS sanctions it (in practice, at the declared estimate) → (3) hospital treats and submits the actual bill → (4) UTI audits and approves → (5) net payment. Module 14 measures the gap between the Stage-2 sanction and the Stage-3 actual bill.

Limitations & Assumptions

• A ratio above 1 is partly structural. The pre-auth sanction covers only the unlisted procedure, while the final bill includes the whole admission (room, nursing, drugs, listed packages). So a bill above sanction is expected; what is anomalous is an extreme and consistent gap across many claims at one hospital.

• Aggregate ratio, not mean-of-ratios. We rank by absolute excess billed and report the aggregate bill/sanction ratio (total bill ÷ total sanction). Per-claim ratios can look far higher because some sanctions are tiny — we avoid that denominator inflation.

• Coverage, not bypass. Only 5.9% of >₹1L claims went through this pre-auth channel — because most are listed-package claims that do not require unlisted-procedure pre-auth. The 1,296,165 'no pre-auth' claims are a coverage observation, NOT a fraud count.

• Identifiers, not verdicts. Hospital/office names are identifiers; a high deduction or ratio is an investigative lead. Every case needs verification before any action.

• Scope. Primary analysis = last five years (Type-2 unlisted_procedure). The legacy pre_auth system (Type-1, 2012–2017) is summarised separately as historical context.

Pre-Auth Coverage & Control Bypass
How much of the high-value claim flow actually passes through pre-auth

Of 1,378,161 high-value claims (>₹1 lakh) in the last five years (₹31,702.55 Cr gross), only 5.9% went through the unlisted-procedure pre-auth channel. The remaining 1,296,165 (₹29,259.12 Cr) carry no pre-auth record.

Read this as coverage, not fraud. ECHS pre-auth (unlisted procedure) is required only for specific non-package procedures; the vast majority of high-value claims are listed-package admissions that legitimately need no pre-auth. The figure simply shows how narrow the pre-auth net is — and therefore how few high-value claims are subject to any pre-treatment cost check.

It held just 2,949 records, of which 97.3% were marked rejected (PA_APPROVED = N) — yet historical analysis showed rejected pre-auths were still settled and paid at almost the same rate as approved ones. Conclusions

The pre-authorization control, as currently operated, does not constrain cost. Across 142,200 pre-auth claims in the last five years, hospitals billed ₹2,143.68 Cr against ₹1,044.58 Cr sanctioned — ₹1,276.37 Cr in excess, with 71.4% of claims billed above their sanction.

The root cause is structural: ECHS sanctions at the hospital's own declared estimate, with no partial sanction, CGHS benchmarking or cost negotiation at Stage 2. The pre-auth therefore functions as an entry token, not a ceiling — and the gap has widened every year. The concentration of excess at a small set of hospitals, and the deliberate clustering of estimates just under scrutiny thresholds, are the clearest leads for audit.

The fix is a financial gate at Stage 2 (partial sanction against CGHS/package rates), backed by post-hoc audit of the top-excess hospitals. As always, these are prioritised leads — each must be confirmed against itemised bills and clinical records before recovery or enforcement.
